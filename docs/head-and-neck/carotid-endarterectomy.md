# Carotid Endarterectomy

## Surgical overview

Surgical removal of atherosclerotic plaque from the carotid bifurcation to reduce stroke risk. Performed via a longitudinal incision along the anterior border of sternocleidomastoid. Can be performed under regional anaesthesia (awake) or general anaesthesia.

## Recommended regional technique

### Superficial (± intermediate) cervical plexus block

**Targets**: C2–C4 superficial branches — greater auricular, lesser occipital, transverse cervical, supraclavicular nerves  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, at the posterior border of sternocleidomastoid at C3–C4 level, deep to the investing fascia  
**Local anaesthetic**: 10–15 mL ropivacaine 0.375–0.5%  
**Onset**: 10–15 minutes

### Advantages of awake CEA

- Allows continuous neurological monitoring (speech, motor function) during carotid cross-clamping — the gold standard for detecting cerebral ischaemia
- Reduces the need for shunting (only shunt if symptoms develop)
- May reduce perioperative stroke, MI, and death (though evidence is debated)

### Alternatives

- **Combined superficial + deep cervical plexus block** — deep block targets the cervical plexus roots between anterior and middle scalene muscles; more complete analgesia but higher complication risk (phrenic nerve palsy, vertebral artery injection, epidural/intrathecal spread)
- **General anaesthesia with superficial cervical plexus block for postoperative analgesia**
- **Surgeon-delivered supplemental local infiltration** — often needed along the carotid sheath even with a good block

## Key anatomy

- Cervical plexus: C1–C4 ventral rami, emerging posterior to the sternocleidomastoid
- Superficial branches emerge at Erb's point (posterior border of SCM, junction of upper and middle thirds)
- Carotid sheath and its contents lie deep to the block — direct infiltration by the surgeon is often required for carotid manipulation pain
- Phrenic nerve (C3–C5) runs on the anterior surface of anterior scalene — at risk with deep cervical plexus block

## Considerations

- Patient must be cooperative and able to lie still for 1–2 hours — careful patient selection
- Supplemental local infiltration by the surgeon is almost always needed, especially along the carotid sheath
- If conversion to GA is required (patient distress, surgical complication), the airway may be difficult due to surgical retraction and oedema
- Superficial block alone is as effective as combined superficial + deep in most studies, with fewer complications
- Bilateral procedures must not be performed simultaneously under regional (bilateral phrenic risk)
