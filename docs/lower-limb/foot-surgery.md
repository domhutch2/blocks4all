# Foot Surgery

## Surgical overview

Elective and emergency foot surgery — hallux valgus correction (bunion surgery), metatarsal osteotomy, midfoot fusion, toe amputation, debridement, tendon repair. Often performed as day case under regional anaesthesia with or without general anaesthesia.

## Recommended regional technique

### Ankle block

**Targets**: Five terminal nerves at the ankle — tibial, deep peroneal, superficial peroneal, sural, and saphenous nerves  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided or landmark-based, circumferential block at the level of the malleoli  
**Local anaesthetic**: 3–5 mL levobupivacaine 0.5% per nerve (total 15–25 mL)  
**Onset**: 15–20 minutes  
**Duration**: 8–16 hours

### Advantages of ankle block for foot surgery

- Simple, safe, and well-tolerated technique
- Can be performed with or without ultrasound
- No motor block above the ankle — patients mobilise more easily
- Excellent for day case foot surgery
- Low complication rate

### Alternatives

- **Popliteal sciatic nerve block ± saphenous nerve block** — provides more reliable and complete anaesthesia of the foot. Better for procedures extending proximal to the ankle or requiring a calf tourniquet. See [Ankle ORIF](./ankle-orif.md).
- **Local infiltration** — surgeon-delivered, suitable for minor procedures (e.g. toenail surgery, small skin lesions)
- **Digital nerve blocks** — for single toe procedures

|---|---|---|

## Key anatomy

- **Tibial nerve** — posterior to the medial malleolus, deep to flexor retinaculum, alongside posterior tibial artery. Supplies the sole of the foot.
- **Deep peroneal nerve** — anterior to the ankle, between extensor hallucis longus and extensor digitorum longus tendons, lateral to the dorsalis pedis artery. Supplies the first web space.
- **Superficial peroneal nerve** — subcutaneous on the anterolateral ankle. Supplies the dorsum of the foot.
- **Sural nerve** — between the lateral malleolus and Achilles tendon. Supplies the lateral foot.
- **Saphenous nerve** — subcutaneous, anterior to the medial malleolus. Supplies the medial ankle.

## Considerations

- Ankle block is classified as a **superficial** block — can generally be performed without cessation of anticoagulants (see [Anticoagulation & Blocks](../quick-reference/anticoagulation.md))
- For more proximal procedures (ankle ORIF, Achilles tendon repair), a popliteal sciatic block provides better coverage
- Warn patients about numbness and difficulty weight-bearing — provide walking aids and fall prevention advice
- Patients can often be discharged with a functioning block — provide written information on limb protection and analgesia for the wear-off period
