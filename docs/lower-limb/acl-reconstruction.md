# ACL Reconstruction

## Surgical overview

Arthroscopic anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction using hamstring autograft, patellar tendon autograft, or allograft. Tourniquet used. Graft harvest site contributes significantly to postoperative pain.

## Recommended regional technique

### Adductor canal block

**Targets**: Saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, mid-thigh, deep to sartorius  
**Local anaesthetic**: 15–20 mL ropivacaine 0.375%  
**Duration**: 12–16 hours

### Advantages over femoral nerve block

- Preserves quadriceps strength — critical for early rehabilitation
- Similar analgesia for anterior knee pain
- Reduced fall risk

### Alternatives

- **Femoral nerve block** — excellent analgesia but causes significant quadriceps weakness; largely superseded by adductor canal block for ACL surgery
- **Local infiltration analgesia (LIA)** — surgeon-delivered intra-articular and periarticular injection; complements peripheral nerve block

## Key anatomy

- Adductor canal lies deep to sartorius in the mid-thigh
- Contains: saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis, femoral artery and vein
- The vastus medialis nerve branch provides sensation to the anterior knee

## Considerations

- Hamstring graft harvest site pain (posteromedial thigh/knee) is not well covered by adductor canal block — consider multimodal adjuncts
- Early mobilisation and physiotherapy are priorities — motor-sparing block is key
- Single-shot block is usually sufficient; catheters are rarely needed
- Combine with paracetamol, NSAIDs, cryotherapy
