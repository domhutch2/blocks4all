# Ankle ORIF

## Surgical overview

Open reduction and internal fixation of ankle fractures — lateral malleolus (fibula), medial malleolus (tibia), posterior malleolus, or combination (bimalleolar/trimalleolar). Tourniquet typically applied to the thigh or calf.

## Recommended regional technique

### Popliteal sciatic nerve block + saphenous nerve block

**Popliteal sciatic block**

**Targets**: Tibial and common peroneal nerves proximal to their bifurcation in the popliteal fossa  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, lateral or posterior approach, at or above the sciatic nerve bifurcation  
**Local anaesthetic**: 20–25 mL ropivacaine 0.5%  
**Onset**: 15–30 minutes (sciatic nerve is large — allow adequate onset time)

**Saphenous nerve block (adductor canal)**

**Targets**: Saphenous nerve — sensory supply to the medial malleolus and medial ankle  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, mid-thigh at the adductor canal  
**Local anaesthetic**: 10 mL ropivacaine 0.375%

### Alternatives

- **Ankle block** — ring block of the 5 terminal nerves at the ankle (tibial, deep peroneal, superficial peroneal, sural, saphenous). Suitable for foot surgery; less reliable for ankle ORIF with proximal incisions.
- **Spinal anaesthesia** — appropriate as primary anaesthetic, particularly for emergency cases

## Consent discussion

| Benefits | Common side effects | Serious complications |
|---|---|---|
| Good pain control; improved day case rate; with/without general anaesthesia | Transient period of non-weight bearing (may need physiotherapy review ± crutches prior to discharge) | — |

## Key anatomy

- Sciatic nerve bifurcates into tibial and common peroneal nerves in the popliteal fossa, typically 5–8 cm above the popliteal crease
- Block proximal to the bifurcation for reliable coverage of both branches
- Saphenous nerve is a pure sensory branch of the femoral nerve — supplies the medial malleolus via the infrapatellar branch

## Considerations

- Sciatic nerve blocks have a slower onset than brachial plexus blocks — allow 30 minutes before assessing
- Popliteal block provides excellent postoperative analgesia (12–24 hours)
- Warn patients about foot drop during block duration — provide walking aids and advise fall prevention
- Thigh tourniquet tolerance requires sedation or GA; calf tourniquet is usually well tolerated with block alone
- Catheter insertion at the popliteal fossa is feasible for prolonged analgesia
