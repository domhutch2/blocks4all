# Caesarean Section

## Surgical overview

Lower segment caesarean section (LSCS) via a Pfannenstiel incision. May be elective or emergency (categories 1–4). Regional anaesthesia is the preferred technique for both mother and baby.

## Recommended regional technique

### Spinal anaesthesia (for elective and category 2–4)

**Level**: L3/4 or L4/5 interspace  
**Local anaesthetic**: Hyperbaric bupivacaine 0.5% 2.2–2.6 mL (11–13 mg)  
**Opioid adjuvant**: Diamorphine 0.3–0.4 mg or preservative-free morphine 0.1–0.15 mg intrathecally  
**Target block height**: T4 to cold/touch  
**Onset**: 5–10 minutes

### For emergency category 1 (immediate threat to life)

**Existing epidural**: Rapid top-up with 15–20 mL lidocaine 2% with adrenaline 1:200,000 ± sodium bicarbonate  
**No epidural in situ**: Spinal anaesthesia if time permits; general anaesthesia if not

### Postoperative analgesia adjuncts

- **TAP block** — useful when intrathecal opioid is omitted or inadequate. Bilateral subcostal or lateral approach. 20 mL ropivacaine 0.375% per side. Provides somatic wound analgesia for 12–18 hours.
- **Quadratus lumborum (QL) block** — posterior or lateral approach. May provide more extensive and longer-lasting analgesia than TAP. 20 mL ropivacaine 0.25–0.375% per side.
- **Wound infiltration / catheter** — surgeon-placed subfascial catheter with local anaesthetic infusion

## Key anatomy

- Spinal anaesthesia at L3/4 is below the conus medullaris (ends at L1/2)
- Pfannenstiel incision: innervated by T11–L1 intercostal and iliohypogastric/ilioinguinal nerves
- TAP block target: between internal oblique and transversus abdominis

## Considerations

- Intrathecal diamorphine/morphine provides excellent analgesia for 12–24 hours post-LSCS — this is the single most effective analgesic intervention
- Monitor for delayed respiratory depression with intrathecal opioids (especially morphine)
- Aortocaval compression: left lateral tilt must be maintained until delivery
- Hypotension is common after spinal — phenylephrine infusion is standard prophylaxis
- NICE and OAA/AAGBI guidelines recommend regional over general anaesthesia wherever possible
