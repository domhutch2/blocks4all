# Laparotomy

## Surgical overview

Midline or transverse laparotomy for bowel resection, emergency laparotomy, hepatobiliary surgery, or gynaecological surgery. One of the most painful surgical procedures — effective regional analgesia is a cornerstone of enhanced recovery.

## Recommended regional technique

### Thoracic epidural

**Level**: T8–T10 for mid/lower abdominal surgery; T6–T8 for upper abdominal surgery  
**Catheter**: Threaded 3–5 cm into the epidural space  
**Infusion**: Ropivacaine 0.2% + fentanyl 2 mcg/mL at 5–10 mL/hr  
**Duration**: Typically maintained for 48–72 hours postoperatively

### Advantages of epidural

- Gold standard for open abdominal surgery analgesia
- Reduces opioid consumption, ileus, and respiratory complications
- Facilitates early mobilisation and return of gut function
- Provides bilateral, continuous, titratable analgesia

### Alternatives

- **Rectus sheath block** — targets terminal branches of T9–T11 intercostal nerves. 15–20 mL ropivacaine 0.375% per side, between rectus abdominis and posterior rectus sheath. Useful for midline incisions when epidural is contraindicated. Catheter insertion allows continuous infusion.
- **TAP block (subcostal + lateral)** — subcostal TAP for supraumbilical coverage; lateral TAP for infraumbilical. 20 mL ropivacaine 0.375% per injection site. Single-shot duration is limited (~12 hours); catheters improve this.
- **Erector spinae plane (ESP) block** — bilateral ESP at T7–T9. Emerging evidence as an epidural alternative. Motor-sparing; potentially less haemodynamic impact.
- **Intrathecal morphine** — 0.1–0.3 mg preservative-free morphine added to spinal anaesthetic. Excellent analgesia for 12–24 hours. Appropriate when epidural is not planned (e.g. laparoscopic-assisted cases).

## Key anatomy

- Thoracic epidural space: narrower than lumbar; steep angulation of spinous processes requires a midline approach with significant cephalad needle tilt
- Rectus sheath: posterior sheath is absent below the arcuate line (below the umbilicus) — relevant for block technique
- TAP: between internal oblique and transversus abdominis; lateral approach at the mid-axillary line

## Considerations

- Epidural contraindicated with coagulopathy, patient refusal, infection at insertion site, or haemodynamic instability
- Epidural failure rate is 10–30% — have a rescue plan (TAP/rectus sheath block, IV PCA)
- For emergency laparotomy, time and haemodynamic status may preclude epidural insertion
- TAP and rectus sheath blocks provide somatic analgesia only (no visceral coverage) — combine with systemic analgesics
- Enhanced recovery protocols (ERAS) strongly recommend regional anaesthesia for open abdominal surgery
