# Contraindications & Consent

## Contraindications

### Absolute

- **Patient refusal**
- **Anaphylaxis** to the local anaesthetic agent

### Relative — risk-benefit assessment required

- **Anticoagulated patient** — see [Anticoagulation & Blocks](./anticoagulation.md) for deep vs superficial classification and ESRA timing
- **Risk of compartment syndrome** — block may mask the clinical signs
- **Significant respiratory disease** — techniques that risk phrenic nerve palsy (e.g. interscalene, supraclavicular) may cause dangerous respiratory compromise
- **Local infection** at the injection site
- **Pre-existing neurological deficit** in the distribution of the planned block — document baseline status before proceeding

## Consent process

### When to discuss

Discussion of regional anaesthetic techniques should happen as early as possible:

- Pre-assessment clinic (elective surgery)
- Plastics trauma clinic / ward 39 pre-assessment
- Surgical clinics for upper limb, lower limb, breast, and vascular access surgery

The technique should be further discussed by the anaesthetist performing the procedure on the day of surgery.

### What to cover

Tailor information to the patient. Cover:

- Rationale and benefits of the proposed technique
- What the procedure involves and what to expect
- Common risks and side effects
- Serious or life-threatening complications
- Any significant risk specific to that patient
- All possible alternatives — the patient must understand these and not feel coerced into regional anaesthesia

### Day surgery patients

For day surgery involving regional anaesthesia, specific instructions must include:

- Practical aspects of the nerve block
- That sedation may be offered but they will not be under general anaesthetic
- **Analgesia cover** for when the block wears off — provide oral analgesia to take home
- **Limb protection** — the limb will be numb and weak until normal sensation and motor function return
- Expected non-threatening physiological changes (e.g. Horner's syndrome with interscalene block)
- **When to seek help** — signs of infection at the injection site, or failure of normal sensation/motor function to return beyond the expected duration of the block

### Written information

Provision of written information is recommended. The RCoA / RA-UK patient information booklet is available, along with specific advice sheets for day case shoulder surgery.

### Documentation

- The consent process should always be documented in the patient's notes
- The AAGBI advises that **signed consent** is required when the block is not part of another related process (i.e. when it is the sole anaesthetic technique, or for standalone acute pain interventions such as rib fracture blocks)
- Acute pain or concurrent opioid use does not necessarily impair mental capacity
- Capacity should be presumed and assessed as per the Mental Capacity Act 2005
- In urgent situations, clinicians must balance ensuring the patient has sufficient opportunity to consider treatment options whilst providing analgesia in a timely manner — document this decision-making clearly

### Alteration of technique on the day

Changing a planned anaesthetic technique immediately prior to induction or during a procedure is **not best practice**. This should only occur in exceptional circumstances (e.g. patient preference changes, pain develops during surgery, an emergency develops).
