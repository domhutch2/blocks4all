# Nerve Injury Follow-up

Pathway for managing unexpected neurological dysfunction after a peripheral nerve block, based on RA-UK/BOA guidelines and NUTH local protocol.

## 48-hour review

All patients receiving a nerve block should be followed up within 48 hours (in person or electronically) to check for neurological dysfunction.

## If new neurological deficits are detected

Signs lasting **more than 48 hours** after a single-shot block, or any new-onset:
- Paraesthesia (numbness/tingling)
- Weakness
- Unexplained excessive pain

### Step 1: Assess

- Inform the responsible surgical and anaesthesia team
- Review medical, anaesthetic, and surgical records
- Assess all functions of the nerve — sensory and motor
- Observe skin for colour changes, swelling; check pulses
- Consider loosening bandages, splitting plaster of Paris, repositioning the limb

### Step 2: Is motor function affected, symptoms progressive, or deficit painful?

**YES — URGENT LESION**

Is the distribution consistent with the block performed?

- **Yes (likely block-related)**: Consider compression of nerve (e.g. haematoma). Seek **urgent** senior surgical and/or neurology consultation. **Immediate/urgent** surgical decompression may be required. Consider **urgent** ultrasound/MRI.
- **No (likely surgery-related)**: Consider compartment syndrome, nerve trauma or compression from suture/bone fragments/haematoma. Seek urgent surgical review.

**NO — NON-URGENT LESION**

Is the distribution consistent with the block performed?

- **Yes (likely block-related)**: Reassure patient. Protect the limb (sling or splint, advise care for numb areas). Consider early involvement of Chronic Pain Team if allodynia or hyperalgesia present (to exclude/treat CRPS). Review in **2–4 weeks** — if deficits persist, consider nerve conduction studies, EMG, MRI and refer for local neurology or nerve injury specialist opinion.
- **No (likely surgery-related)**: Consider positional pressure-related nerve injury, damage at the surgical site, or prolonged/excessive tourniquet. Refer to surgical team.

:::tip Neuropathic pain
If neuropathic pain is present or pain is limited to the distribution of a particular nerve, refer to the Acute Pain Service and consider starting neuropathic medication.
:::
