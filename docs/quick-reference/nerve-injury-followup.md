# Nerve Injury Follow-up

Pathway for managing unexpected neurological dysfunction after a peripheral nerve block, based on RA-UK/BOA guidelines and local protocol (v3, March 2024).

Nerve injury is a recognised but rare complication of regional anaesthesia. Most symptoms resolve in 4–6 weeks in over 95% of patients, and in 99% by one year.

## Mechanisms of nerve injury

- **Vascular injury** — haematoma at the surgical or block site, disruption of blood supply, DVT. Presents early (minutes to hours).
- **Compression** — expanding haematoma, tourniquet/casts, compartment syndrome, improper positioning, increased endoneural fluid pressure after block, trauma/surgery.
- **Direct needle trauma** — frequency is greater with long-bevelled needles; duration and severity are greater with short-bevelled needles.
- **Intraneural injection** — increases pressure inside the nerve, causing damage.
- **LA neurotoxicity** — all local anaesthetics are potentially neurotoxic. Vasoconstrictive additives may affect neural blood supply and potentiate injury.

:::info Confounding factors
Pre-existing neuropathies (e.g. diabetes), demyelinating diseases (e.g. MS), and peripheral vascular disease may make nerves more susceptible to injury. Document any pre-existing neurological deficits before performing a block.
:::

## Types of nerve injury

| Type | Description | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Neuropraxia** | Axons and supporting connective tissue intact | Complete recovery expected within weeks to months |
| **Axonotmesis** | Axonal interruption with conservation of connective tissue | Generally favourable, although not always complete |
| **Neurotmesis** | Complete fascicular disruption | Often poor, even with prompt surgical intervention |

## 48-hour review

All patients receiving a nerve block should be followed up within 48 hours (in person or electronically) to check for neurological dysfunction.

### Pre-existing deficits

At the 48-hour review, compare post-operative findings with any pre-existing neurological symptoms documented before the block. If pre-operative and post-operative deficits are consistent, document that the deficit was pre-existing.

## If new neurological deficits are detected

Signs lasting **more than 48 hours** after a single-shot block, or any new-onset:
- Paraesthesia (numbness/tingling)
- Weakness
- Unexplained excessive pain

### Step 1: Assess

- Inform the responsible surgical and anaesthesia team
- Review medical, anaesthetic, and surgical records
- Assess all functions of the nerve — sensory and motor
- Observe skin for colour changes, swelling; check pulses
- Consider loosening bandages, splitting plaster of Paris, repositioning the limb

### Step 2: Is motor function affected, symptoms progressive, or deficit painful?

**YES — URGENT LESION**

Is the distribution consistent with the block performed?

- **Yes (likely block-related)**: Consider compression of nerve (e.g. haematoma). Seek **urgent** senior surgical and/or neurology consultation. **Immediate/urgent** surgical decompression may be required. Consider **urgent** ultrasound/MRI.
- **No (likely surgery-related)**: Consider compartment syndrome, nerve trauma or compression from suture/bone fragments/haematoma. Seek urgent surgical review.

**NO — NON-URGENT LESION**

Is the distribution consistent with the block performed?

- **Yes (likely block-related)**: Reassure patient. Protect the limb (sling or splint, advise care for numb areas). Consider early involvement of Chronic Pain Team if allodynia or hyperalgesia present (to exclude/treat CRPS). Review in **2–4 weeks** — if deficits persist, consider nerve conduction studies, EMG, MRI and refer for local neurology or nerve injury specialist opinion.
- **No (likely surgery-related)**: Consider positional pressure-related nerve injury, damage at the surgical site, or prolonged/excessive tourniquet. Refer to surgical team.

:::tip Neuropathic pain
If neuropathic pain is present or pain is limited to the distribution of a particular nerve, refer to the Acute Pain Service and consider starting neuropathic medication (e.g. gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline).
:::

:::info Differential diagnosis
Post-surgical inflammatory neuropathy should be considered in the differential for both urgent and non-urgent lesions.
:::
