# Hand & Plastics Trauma

## Surgical overview

Emergency and elective hand surgery — tendon repair, fracture fixation, nerve repair, replantation, debridement, skin grafting, flap coverage. Often performed as day case under regional anaesthesia alone.

## Recommended regional technique

### Plan A: Axillary brachial plexus block

**Targets**: Terminal branches of the brachial plexus — median, ulnar, radial, and musculocutaneous nerves  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, at the axilla, identifying individual nerves around the axillary artery  
**Local anaesthetic**: 20–30 mL levobupivacaine 0.25–0.5% (divided between individual nerves)  
**Onset**: 15–20 minutes  
**Duration**: 8–16 hours

### Advantages of axillary block for hand trauma

- No risk of pneumothorax or phrenic nerve palsy
- Individual nerves can be selectively targeted — useful when only specific distributions are needed
- Well tolerated awake — ideal for day case surgery
- May include "top-up blocks" if coverage is incomplete

### Plan B: alternatives

- **Supraclavicular brachial plexus block** — provides reliable hand and forearm anaesthesia ("spinal of the upper limb"). Small risk of phrenic nerve palsy (~1%) and pneumothorax.
- **Infraclavicular brachial plexus block** — good for catheter placement if prolonged surgery or postoperative infusion is anticipated. Pneumothorax risk slightly higher than axillary.
- **WALANT** — for simple procedures (e.g. trigger finger, carpal tunnel, tendon repair) where no tourniquet is needed. See [Carpal Tunnel Release](./carpal-tunnel-release.md).
- **Wrist blocks** — targeted median, ulnar, and/or radial nerve blocks at the wrist for limited procedures.
- **Digital nerve blocks** — for single finger procedures.

## Consent discussion (NUTH)

| Benefits | Common side effects | Serious complications |
|---|---|---|
| High patient satisfaction; good pain control; increased day case rates; follow-up via text message service | Horner's (supraclavicular only); phrenic nerve block (supraclavicular only); tourniquet pain | Pneumothorax (supraclavicular and infraclavicular) |

## Key anatomy

- Axillary block: median nerve lies superficial/lateral to axillary artery, ulnar nerve superficial/medial, radial nerve deep/posterior, musculocutaneous nerve within coracobrachialis
- Intercostobrachial nerve (T2) — provides sensation to medial upper arm; not covered by brachial plexus block. Requires separate subcutaneous infiltration if tourniquet is used on the upper arm.

## Considerations

- Axillary block is the safest brachial plexus approach — no pneumothorax, no phrenic palsy
- For complex or prolonged hand surgery, consider an infraclavicular catheter for ongoing analgesia
- Tourniquet tolerance: forearm tourniquet is well tolerated under brachial plexus block; upper arm tourniquet may require intercostobrachial nerve supplement or sedation
- Patients can often be discharged with a functioning block — provide written information on limb protection and analgesia for the "wear-off" period
