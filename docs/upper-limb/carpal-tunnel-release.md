# Carpal Tunnel Release

## Surgical overview

Open or endoscopic division of the transverse carpal ligament (flexor retinaculum) to decompress the median nerve. Usually a short day-case procedure.

## Recommended regional technique

### WALANT (Wide Awake Local Anaesthesia No Tourniquet)

**Technique**: Subcutaneous infiltration of local anaesthetic with adrenaline at the surgical site  
**Local anaesthetic**: 10–20 mL lidocaine 1% with 1:100,000 adrenaline  
**Onset**: Inject 30 minutes prior to incision (allows adrenaline vasoconstriction to provide a bloodless field)  
**Duration**: 2–3 hours

### Advantages of WALANT

- No sedation, no tourniquet, no block-related motor weakness
- Patient can demonstrate active finger flexion intraoperatively to confirm complete decompression
- Very low complication rate
- Cost-effective — can be performed outside main theatre

### Alternatives

- **Median nerve block at the wrist** — 3–5 mL lidocaine 2% between palmaris longus and flexor carpi radialis at the proximal wrist crease
- **Axillary brachial plexus block** — if bilateral or combined with other hand procedures
- **IV regional anaesthesia (Bier's block)** — effective but requires tourniquet and monitoring setup

## Key anatomy

- Median nerve enters the hand deep to the flexor retinaculum between tendons of FDS and FCR
- Palmar cutaneous branch arises proximal to the carpal tunnel — not protected by the retinaculum
- Recurrent motor branch to thenar muscles — variable anatomy, at risk during surgery

## Considerations

- WALANT is now the standard of care in many centres for carpal tunnel release
- Adrenaline in fingers is safe — the old teaching that it causes digital ischaemia has been disproven
- No need for preoperative fasting with WALANT (no sedation)
- Bilateral procedures can be done in one sitting
