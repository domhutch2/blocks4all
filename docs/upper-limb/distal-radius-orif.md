# ORIF Distal Radius

## Surgical overview

Open reduction and internal fixation of distal radius fractures — volar locking plate via a volar (Henry) approach. One of the most common orthopaedic procedures.

## Recommended regional technique

### Supraclavicular brachial plexus block

**Targets**: Trunks/divisions of the brachial plexus at the supraclavicular fossa  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, in-plane lateral-to-medial, needle directed toward the "corner pocket" (junction of first rib and subclavian artery)  
**Local anaesthetic**: 20–25 mL ropivacaine 0.5%  
**Onset**: 10–15 minutes  
**Duration**: 12–18 hours

### Alternatives

- **Axillary brachial plexus block** — avoids risk of pneumothorax and phrenic nerve palsy; ideal when these are concerns
- **Distal nerve blocks at the wrist** — median, ulnar, and radial nerve blocks for targeted analgesia without proximal motor block; useful for postoperative analgesia alone (not suitable as primary surgical anaesthetic for ORIF)

## Key anatomy

- Supraclavicular fossa: brachial plexus seen as a cluster of hypoechoic nodules ("bunch of grapes") lateral and posterior to the subclavian artery, above the first rib
- Pleura visible deep to the first rib — key safety landmark
- Dorsal scapular artery may traverse the plexus

## Considerations

- Supraclavicular block provides reliable hand and wrist anaesthesia — "the spinal of the upper limb"
- Tourniquet tolerance is usually good for forearm tourniquets
- Avoid supraclavicular approach in patients with significant respiratory disease (phrenic nerve palsy risk ~1%)
- Can be performed as day-case regional anaesthesia without general anaesthesia
