# Elbow Surgery

## Surgical overview

Elbow arthroscopy, ORIF of distal humerus/olecranon fractures, total elbow replacement, tennis elbow release, ulnar nerve transposition. Surgery may involve a tourniquet on the upper arm.

## Recommended regional technique

### Supraclavicular brachial plexus block

**Targets**: Trunks/divisions of the brachial plexus at the supraclavicular fossa — provides reliable coverage of the entire upper limb below the shoulder  
**Approach**: Ultrasound-guided, in-plane lateral-to-medial  
**Local anaesthetic**: 20–25 mL levobupivacaine 0.5%  
**Onset**: 10–15 minutes  
**Duration**: 12–18 hours

### Alternatives

- **Interscalene brachial plexus block** — covers the elbow well (C5–C7) but may miss ulnar nerve (C8–T1) territory. Useful when shoulder and elbow are both involved. Risk of phrenic nerve palsy.
- **Infraclavicular brachial plexus block** — reliable coverage of all terminal branches. Good option for catheter placement.
- **Axillary brachial plexus block** — targets individual terminal nerves; no pneumothorax risk. May be preferred if the surgical site is distal on the forearm/elbow.

## Consent discussion

| Benefits | Common side effects | Serious complications |
|---|---|---|
| Improved day case rate; athletic/sports injury patients may wish to watch surgery | Horner's syndrome (supraclavicular/interscalene); phrenic nerve block (supraclavicular/interscalene); tourniquet pain | Pneumothorax (supraclavicular and infraclavicular) |

## Key anatomy

- Elbow joint innervation: musculocutaneous, median, radial, and ulnar nerves
- Supraclavicular block reliably covers all four nerves
- Interscalene block may spare the inferior trunk (C8–T1) — ulnar nerve territory (medial elbow, little finger)
- Intercostobrachial nerve (T2) provides medial upper arm sensation — not covered by brachial plexus block; may need supplementation if an upper arm tourniquet is used

## Considerations

- Supraclavicular is often called the "spinal of the upper limb" — most reliable single-injection technique for complete arm coverage
- For ulnar nerve surgery, ensure the block provides adequate ulnar nerve coverage — supraclavicular or infraclavicular approaches are more reliable than interscalene for this
- Upper arm tourniquet tolerance may require intercostobrachial nerve supplement or sedation
- Patients can often be discharged with a functioning block — provide written information on limb protection
